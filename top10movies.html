The Ten
Best Movies
Ever Made

10b. Something Wild

It's hard to label Something Wild as any one thing because it's one of the most sophisticated and daring films of all time to defy genre. It's a raunchy comedy that turns into a road movie that turns into a romance that turns into a dark and violent thriller. Jeff Daniels and Melanie Griffith both give fantastic lead performances, but the late, great Ray Liotta, in his breakout role, steals the show. His entrance signifies what is quite possibly the best and most effective tonal shift ever to be found in a mainstream American movie. This is the late, great Jonathan Demme's best directorial effort amongst a career's worth of wonderful films.

10a. Die Hard

Simply put, Die Hard is the best action movie ever made. Period. It has more heart, humor, and thrills than any of the action titles that came before and after it. John McTiernan's direction is stylish, classy, and refined, elevating the movie above its genre significantly. Make no mistake, Die Hard is an action movie through and through, it's just made with more care, artistry, and humanity than any other film of its kind.

10. True Romance

True Romance is the first screenplay that Quentin Tarantion ever wrote and the second that he had produced (after his writing and directing debut with 1992's Reservoir Dogs). It's an endlessly entertaining, deeply romantic, and hyper-violent depiction of young love. Part of what makes it so great is that it combines Tarantimo's indie sensibilities with director Tony Scott's (Top Gun, Days of Thunder) commercial tastes. The result is one of the most creatively thrilling movies ever made.

9. Robocop

Paul Verhoeven's entry into mainstream American filmmaking remains one of the smartest, most provocative, and most ingenious movies ever made. Robocop works as a science fiction action/thriller and as a profound piece of social commentary that examines corporational greed, the ethical dangers that can come with advancing technology, and the essence of what it means to be human. Robocop is the rare movie that entertains every bit as much as it enlightens.

8. Raising Arizona

Raising Arizona is one of the best comedies not only of its time but of all time. It's the Coen Borthers at their quirkiest and most absurd, but it's also them at their most astute and emotionally moving. Raising Arizona is like a Looney Tunes cartoon made for adults. It's silly and absurd, but also wholly sophisticated with wonderous moments of whimsey and sincere moments that honestly depict the human condition. Nicolas Cage and Holly Hunter, both early in their careers whent they made the film, are at their aboslute best--no small feat for either of their enormous talents.

7. Heathers

Screenwriter Daniel Waters and director Michael Lehmann gave the high school experience the ultimate representation with this wickedly dark, bitingly satiric, and deeply (though disturbingly) funny film. Heathers captures the true feelings of high school through its dark and demented storyline--which centers on a young couple (Christian Slater and Winona Ryder) who start offing the popular kids at school while making it look like suicide. John Hughes never touched on what it truly feels like to be a teenager the way that Heathers did.

6. Natural Born Killers

Oliver Stone's bombastic, chaotic, and insanely innovative Natural Born Killers is the ultimate 90s satire. It shines a light on television culture and the media's romanticization of violence. The challenging film thrills its audience in the moment with its depictions of violence, only to make them question its consquences and examine their own relationshp with it afterwards. Stylistically, the film is one of the most groundbreaking movies ever made. Its experiments with photography, editing, animation, and digital effects were and continue to be far ahead of their time.

5. Brazil

Terry Gilliam's Brazil is a nighmarishly Kafkaesque and dreamily surreal film that thoughfully examines the feelings and consequences associated with working in a repressive bureaucratic enviornment. Brazil is about the importance of holding onto dreams and ideals that lift us out of the inhumanity and monotony of everyday life. As Gilliam has said in many interviews, the film is about being so trapped in an unwanted situation that the only way to escape it is through the relief of going completely insane.

4. Blue Velvet

David Lynch's definitive masterpiece explores the underside of small-town America. It is a strange and mind-blowing thriller/mystery that is as shocking and disturbing today as it was when it was released back in 1986. Lynch brought surrealism to popular culture with this film in a way that had never before been done and hasn't been done since. Simply put, there is nothing quite like Blue Velvet. To this day, Dennis Hopper's portrayal of the psychotic Frank Booth remains as one of the best villains ever put on film.

3. Wild at Heart

David Lynch's free-form adaptation of Barry Gifford's wonderful novel, Wild at Heart, is a violent, funny, colorful, exciting, and sexy romp. It is the single most entertaining and thoroughly whacked out movie of Lynch's career--and that says a lot. Nicolas Cage and Laura Dern star as a crazy-in-love couple who go on the run from the law, disturbed family members, and dangerous hitmen. Wild at Heart is, simply put, one of the wildest and most inventive road movies of all time.

2. Edward Scissorhands

Tim Burton's classic is the definitive movie about lonelieness and alienation. Edward Scissorhands (portrayed by Johnny Deep) is the ultimate outsider--someone who literally can't touch anything without destroyig it. He is a character who symbolizes racial discrimination, sexism, prejudice towards those with disabilities, and anyone else who is mistreated for being a little different. Edward Scissorhands is a modern-day fairy tale that teaches us an important lesson about the treatment of those who don't (or are unable to) fit in with social norms. It is a beautifully sad and heartbreakingly honest film about what it feels like to be an outsider.

1. Blade Runner

Ridley Scott's Blade Runner is arguably the most influential movie of the past forty years. At the very least, it's one of them, as it has influenced countless other films, music videos, commercials, and comics since its release. It is the most obsessively and beautifully detailed science fiction film since Stanley Kubrick's 2001, and it arguably even goes further than that undisputed classic. Blade Runner's designs are so meticulously executed that they allow you to make surprising new discoveries with each new viewing of the film--regardless of how many times you've seen it. Blade Runner is, quite simply, the most beautifully and painstakingly crafted film of all time.
